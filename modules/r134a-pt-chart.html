Understanding the PT Chart
This pressure-temperature chart shows the relationship between pressure and temperature for R134a refrigerant at 5000ft altitude. Use this chart to determine saturation temperatures for superheat and subcooling calculations.
🏔️ Altitude Correction

This chart is corrected for 5000ft altitude where atmospheric pressure is approximately 12.2 PSI instead of 14.7 PSI at sea level. Always use altitude-corrected charts for accurate readings.

How to Use This Chart
Follow these steps to use the PT chart for superheat and subcooling calculations.
1
For Superheat Calculations
  1. Measure low-side pressure with your gauge
  2. Find the pressure in the chart
  3. Read the corresponding saturation temperature
  4. Subtract from actual suction line temperature

Example: 35 PSI = 42°F saturation temperature

2
For Subcooling Calculations
  1. Measure high-side pressure with your gauge
  2. Find the pressure in the chart
  3. Read the corresponding saturation temperature
  4. Subtract actual liquid line temperature

Example: 220 PSI = 115°F saturation temperature

Altitude Correction Factors
How altitude affects pressure readings and calculations.
🏔️ Why Altitude Matters

At 5000ft, atmospheric pressure is about 2.5 PSI lower than sea level. This means gauge pressures read slightly lower, but the absolute pressure relationships remain the same.

Altitude Atmospheric Pressure Effect on Gauge Readings Correction Needed
Sea Level 14.7 PSI Standard readings None
5000ft 12.2 PSI ~2.5 PSI lower Use altitude-corrected chart
10000ft 10.1 PSI ~4.6 PSI lower Use high-altitude chart
